# Resolution — Adjudicating Outcomes

**No dice.** Outcomes are decided by **DM judgment**, grounded in the fiction and the
character sheet. This file is how that judgment stays fair and consistent.

---

## The Four Inputs

When the player attempts something with an uncertain outcome, weigh:

1. **Plausibility of the plan.** Does it make sense in the fiction? Is it clever,
   prepared, well-reasoned — or careless and hopeful? A smart plan with the right
   leverage should *substantially* improve the odds, even for a weaker character. This
   is the player's main lever, so reward good thinking.
2. **The character.** Their relevant **attribute** (raw ceiling) and **skill
   proficiency** (training), plus traits and gear. **This is the dominant factor** for
   anything requiring real capability.
3. **The opposition / difficulty.** How hard is this, who or what resists, what are the
   conditions (terrain, time pressure, numbers, the NPC's own skill)?
4. **Luck.** A *small* wobble (`attributes.md`). Use it to break ties, occasionally
   bless a long shot, or soften a disaster — **never** to override a clear skill
   mismatch or an absurd plan.

**Weighting:** skill and traits first, plan second, luck a distant last. A master
swordsman wins ordinary fights without drama; a novice needs a genuinely clever edge.

---

## The Outcome Spectrum

Don't resolve as binary pass/fail. Place the result on a spectrum and narrate it:

- **Clean success** — it works, no strings. (Use when capability clearly exceeds the task.)
- **Success at a cost** — it works, but something is spent, broken, noticed, or owed.
- **Partial / mixed** — part of the goal lands; complications arise.
- **Failure with a consequence** — it doesn't work, and the situation worsens
  meaningfully (but a path forward remains — *fail forward*).
- **Disaster** — for reckless plans against steep odds, or the rare cruel break of bad
  luck. Real, lasting cost. (Never PC death — see CLAUDE.md §5.)

Prefer results that **change the situation** and **keep momentum**. Even failure should
hand the player a new problem to act on, not a dead end.

---

## Pacing the Checks (ties to CLAUDE.md §2)

- **High-action:** resolve in **short beats** and pause often; one exchange of a fight,
  one line of a tense negotiation, then back to the player.
- **Low-stakes:** resolve the **whole attempt** at once and narrate the result.
- Don't call for a "check" on trivial things a competent person would simply do — just
  narrate success. Reserve adjudication for genuinely uncertain moments.

## Difficulty Reference (for the DM's internal calibration)

| Difficulty | A character succeeds reliably if they are… |
|---|---|
| Trivial | Anyone, basically. Don't adjudicate. |
| Easy | Novice in the skill, or a relevant attribute ≥10. |
| Moderate | Apprentice, or attribute ~14, or a solid plan. |
| Hard | Journeyman, or attribute ~16–18, or a clever well-prepared plan. |
| Very Hard | Expert, or attribute ~20+, or an excellent plan with real leverage. |
| Formidable | Master, or a brilliant plan exploiting a true weakness. |
| Near-Impossible | Grandmaster / Mythic capability, or a perfect plan plus a Luck break. |

A great plan can effectively lower the difficulty a step or two; a foolish one raises it.

---

## Player Questions & Knowledge Gates

The player may ask you things. **Answer as their character would know.**
- If the character would plausibly know or perceive it → tell them.
- If they wouldn't → say so in-world ("You couldn't say" / "Only rumors") rather than
  revealing hidden lore. Offer that they *could* find out by acting (asking around,
  investigating, traveling).
- Never leak `dm-secrets/` to be helpful. Discovery is part of the game.

When the player **earns** a piece of hidden knowledge, promote it to the public files
and note the in-game date (CLAUDE.md §8).
