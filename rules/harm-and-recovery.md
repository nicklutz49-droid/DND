# Harm, Health & Recovery

> How injury, survival, and exhaustion work in a **no-dice** game. Injury itself is **not
> tracked on a meter** — the DM judges and narrates it from the fiction (`resolution.md`).
> Only **Fatigue** gets a light explicit track. Above all: the player character can be
> broken, but **never permanently killed** (CLAUDE.md §5).

---

## 1. Harm Is Narrated, Not Counted

There are **no hit points and no wound ladder.** When someone is hurt, the DM weighs the
fiction — the weapon, the surprise, the armor, the attacker's skill, the four inputs of
`resolution.md` — and **narrates an injury that fits**, then lets it shape what follows.

- **Injuries are consistent and persistent.** A spear through the thigh doesn't vanish next
  scene: it bleeds, it slows, it can fail at the worst moment. The DM remembers what's hurt
  and lets it weigh on later judgment (a gashed sword-arm makes Blades harder).
- **Severity is a spectrum**, matching the outcome spectrum: a graze that only stings, a
  solid wound that hampers, a grievous one that drops a person, a killing blow. Name it
  plainly so the player can react.
- **Position and protection matter.** Armor, cover, reach, footing, and numbers all feed the
  DM's judgment of how much harm lands — describe them and they count.

> Rule of thumb: don't ask "how many points?" Ask "**how bad, and what does it cost them
> now?**" — then make the next beat honor the answer.

---

## 2. The Player Character at Death's Door

The PC's story is too valuable to end on one bad beat. A blow that **would kill or finish an
ordinary person** instead drops the player into a **costly fork** — *beaten, not buried*:

- **Captured** — they wake disarmed in enemy hands (a new arc: prison, ransom, escape).
- **Left for dead** — they come to later, robbed and alone, the enemy gone, the wound deep.
- **Grievously marked** — they live, but carry it: a lasting scar, a ruined hand, a limp, a
  haunting (record it on the sheet; let it drive the story).
- **Saved at a price** — an ally, a stranger, or a bargain pulls them back — and now they owe.
- **Broken and routed** — they escape with their life but lose the field, a holding, their
  gear, or a companion left behind.

Make these moments **feel earned and ruinous** — a brush with death should cost dearly and
never feel cheap. But the curtain never falls on the PC. Failure forks the story; it never
ends it.

> **This shield is the player's alone.** NPCs, allies, and enemies are mortal and **can die**
> — and the player can lose people they care about. That contrast is what gives the PC's own
> survival its weight.

## 3. Recovery & Healing

Wounds mend with **time and care**, not magic-on-tap:

| Severity | Rough mending time (with rest + care) |
|---|---|
| Bruises, grazes, a beating | hours to a couple of days |
| A real wound (cut, break, deep blow) | one to several weeks; hampered until then |
| Grievous (near-fatal, lost function) | many weeks to months; may leave a lasting mark |

- **Care speeds and secures it.** Rest, food, warmth, and the **Medicine** skill (or a
  healer's kit) shorten recovery and stave off the bad turn. Neglect invites **infection,
  fever, or a wound that sets wrong** — a failure-with-a-consequence the DM can play.
- **Magical healing is real but rare** — costly, guarded, never a casual top-up. It can save
  a life or knit a grievous wound, but it exacts its own price (see the magic traditions,
  `world/world-bible.md` §5). Don't let it trivialize injury.
- **Lasting marks stay** until specifically resolved (great care, rare magic, or a story
  beat). They live on the character sheet and are fair game as hooks.

## 4. Fatigue (the one tracked meter)

Exhaustion *is* tracked, on a simple ladder — because it builds predictably and the frontier
(winter, distance, sleepless nights) should bite. **Constitution** governs how well a
character resists sliding down it.

**Fresh → Tired → Exhausted → Spent**

- **What pushes you down:** a forced march, a long fight or chase, heavy labor, exposure and
  cold, hunger or thirst, going without sleep, and **sustained or strenuous magic.**
- **What it costs:** each step makes physical and mental effort **harder** (the DM weighs it
  like any penalty). **Exhausted** characters are clumsy, slow, and easily hurt; **Spent**
  means collapse — they must stop, and pushing on risks real harm.
- **Clearing it:** step back up with solid rest, a hot meal, warmth, and safety. **Spent →
  Fresh** takes a proper night's sleep (or more) in decent conditions — not a brief breather.

> **Fatigue and harm feed each other.** A worn-down character takes wounds more easily, and a
> grievous wound can leave someone Exhausted on its own. Track Fatigue; narrate harm; let the
> two color each other.

---

## 5. Quick DM Checklist
- Hurt? **Name the injury and its cost now** — don't count points.
- Keep wounds **persistent**; let them weigh on later judgment.
- PC "dies"? **Pick a fork** from §2 — costly, earned, never final.
- Healing is **time + care**; magic is rare and priced.
- Update the sheet: lasting marks, and the current **Fatigue** step.
