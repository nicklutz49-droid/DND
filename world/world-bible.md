# The World — World Bible

> The campaign's **canonical, public lore**: what is broadly known or knowable about the
> world. This is a **living document** — expand it as the campaign develops and as the
> player earns hidden knowledge (promoted here from `dm-secrets/` with the in-game date).
> Game mechanics live in `rules/`; what the player doesn't know yet lives in `dm-secrets/`.
>
> Sections marked **▸ To develop** are deliberate open seams for future worldbuilding.

---

## 1. The Shape of the World — Four Continents

The known world is **four continents**:

- **Acente** — the contested central continent and **the stage of this campaign**: the
  "New World." Humans fled here when their homeland died and forged their **Empire**;
  **elf and dwarf nations and city-states** are scattered across it; and the wild interior
  already belongs to **native goblin kingdoms, dragonborn city-states, and countless
  decentralized tribes**. Still being explored and fought over — **this is the frontier.**
- **The Frozen North** — the **abandoned** human homeland, no longer habitable. *Why it
  died is not commonly known.* (Buried mystery — see `dm-secrets/`.)
- **Kharak** — homeland of **dwarves, gnomes, halflings, and dragonborn**. Harsh,
  mountainous, draconic.
- **Saal Aethal** — homeland of **elves, lizardfolk**, and other tropical kin. Warm,
  jungled, old.

**▸ To develop:** a rough map and seas; travel/distance between continents; the specific
**frontier region of Acente** where play begins (the cartographer will build it); climate
and terrain of the starting region.

## 2. The Peoples

Many kinds share the world. **Humans, elves, and dwarves are by far the most common**;
beyond them is a long tail of rarer kin.

- **The common kin:** Humans, Elves, Dwarves.
- **The wider kin:** Gnomes, Halflings, Dragonborn, Lizardfolk, Goblins, Gnolls, Kobolds,
  Orcs — *and more besides.* None are mere "monsters": each is a **full people** with its
  own language, faith, art, and politics. "Monster" is a settler's slur, not a truth.

**How peoples sit relative to the three powers (see §4):**
- **Humans** built and lead the **Empire** of Acente (newcomers from the dead North).
- **Elves** (from Saal Aethal) and **Dwarves** (from Kharak) hold their **own independent
  nations and city-states** across Acente — old powers, not imperial subjects.
- The frontier's **native peoples** — goblin kingdoms, dragonborn city-states, and the
  decentralized tribes of many kinds — already hold the land the newcomers covet.

**▸ To develop:** distinct culture/appearance entries per people; sub-races; relations and
old grudges between specific peoples; where the rarer kin concentrate.

## 3. The Divine & the Spirits

- **The gods are silent.** They never answer plainly, and no one can prove they are truly
  there. Yet **those who pray to them wield real power** — so it is genuinely **ambiguous**
  whether a devout person channels a god's gift or simply their own latent magic. Faith is
  socially powerful and personally real; certainty is not on offer.
- **Elemental spirits** run much of the world's background — **local powers of place**
  (river, stone, storm, hearth, grove). They are real and can be dealt with, but **not
  everyone is knowledgeable about them**; shamans, hedge-folk, and many native peoples are.
- The **Empire** venerates a structured **imperial pantheon** through a **state priesthood**
  (see §4), and dismisses frontier spirit-worship as backward.

**▸ To develop:** the named gods of the imperial pantheon and their domains; rival/older
faiths; how tribal spirit-ways actually work; holy days.

## 4. The Three Powers of the Frontier

The frontier is pressed between three forces in motion. **None is a monolith.** (Player
standing and specific named factions are tracked in `world/factions.md`.)

### The Empire of Acente
A **vast, bureaucratic conqueror** — emperor, deep bureaucracy, legions, magistrates,
roads, and taxes. Civilization as a patient machine, hungry for the frontier.
- **Faith:** an **imperial pantheon** with a state priesthood; orthodoxy matters.
- **Magic:** **licensed and controlled** — wizards trained and sanctioned through academies
  and registries; **unsanctioned magic (pacts, spirit-work) is outlawed and hunted.**
- **The old nations:** treats the **elf and dwarf realms of Acente as independent rival
  powers** — not subjects. Acente is a contest of several great powers, not just Empire
  vs. frontier.
- **Its true design** hums beneath the surface story — see `dm-secrets/empire-metaplot.md`.

### The Native Peoples
The frontier's first inhabitants: **great nations** (goblin kingdoms, dragonborn
city-states) alongside **countless decentralized tribes** of many kinds. They feud, ally,
trade, and war among themselves — and the land is already theirs.

### The Rival Settlers
Independent colonists, chartered companies, and opportunists racing to stake claims and
build before the Empire swallows it all.

**▸ To develop:** the Empire's name, emperor, and provincial structure; specific elf/dwarf
nations; named goblin kingdoms and dragonborn city-states; major settler companies.

## 5. Magic

Magic is **woven into the world** — in principle **anyone can wield it**, but it demands
**skill and hard-won knowledge**, so true practitioners stay **uncommon**, and the deeper
arts stay **feared**. It is **understated**, not a fireworks show. (Mechanics: see
`rules/skills.md` › *Magic*.)

There are **several paths to power**, each behaving like its own learned tradition:
- **Study / Wizardry** — the **most common** road: disciplined learning of how the world's
  magic works. Governed by intellect.
- **Pacts / Warlockry** — power **bargained** from a patron (a fey lord, a devil, an elder
  power) at a price. Dreaded, and outlawed by the Empire.
- **Faith / Divine** — power received by **devotion**, shaped by the nature of the god
  prayed to (see §3's ambiguity).
- **Innate / Planar-born** — those **born with a connection** to a deeper plane, who tap
  its magic directly (sorcerous, often inherited).
- **Spirit-work** — dealing with the **elemental spirits** of place (the shaman's/animist's
  way), common among native peoples.

**▸ To develop:** the costs and tells of each tradition; how pacts bind; named magics as
they appear in play.

## 6. The Cosmology — Near Worlds & Far Worlds

The mortal world is brushed by other realms arranged in **two tiers**:

- **The Near Worlds** press close against **wild, untamed land** — so on a raw frontier like
  this they are **only a step away**. They are why the frontier is thick with magic and
  danger:
  - **The Fey Otherworld** — realm of the **fey courts** (see §7).
  - **The spirit & elemental world** — home of the powers of place (see §3).
  - **The realm of the restless dead** — where souls linger before they pass or are claimed.
- **The Far Worlds** lie across an **almost-impassable gulf**, reachable only rarely and at
  terrible cost — which is why the gods are silent and fiendish pacts are so dreaded:
  - **The heavens of the silent gods.**
  - **The Hells** — domain of lawful **devils** and chaos **demons** (see §8).
  - **The outer dark** beyond.

**Souls** are **coveted currency**: when a mortal dies, the powers **compete for the dead**.
This is why bargaining a soul away is the ultimate dread — and why devils traffic in them.
The exact mechanics of the soul's fate are a **mystery** to mortals.

**▸ To develop:** how the living cross into the Near Worlds (thin places, rites); named
regions of each realm; what the dead experience.

## 7. The Fey

The fey dwell in an **Otherworld pressed against the wilderness** — and because this
frontier is **wild**, the fey are **near**. They are organized into **courts**, **alien in
morality**, and **bound by bargains and old rules** that even they cannot break. They are
neither friends nor foes by default; they are *other*. A promise made to them is a real and
dangerous thing.

**▸ To develop:** the structure of the courts (seasonal? Seelie/Unseelie? something native
to this world?); notable fey lords; the binding rules of fey bargains; the fey's stake in
the frontier's fate.

---

## 8. Fiends — Devils & Demons

The lower Far Worlds hold **two distinct powers**, and they may **war with one another**:
- **Devils** — **lawful**: ordered **hierarchies**, **contracts**, and **soul-bargains**.
  They corrupt **patiently** and **by consent**. These are the classic **pact-patrons** of
  warlocks: they give you exactly what you asked for, and collect exactly what you owe.
- **Demons** — **raw chaos**: they want only to **destroy and unmake**. No bargains, no
  order — appetite without end.

Both are **Far** and therefore **rare**; reaching or summoning them is the work of the
desperate or the damned, and the Empire hunts those who try.

**▸ To develop:** the infernal hierarchy and any named archdevils; the nature and origin of
demons; how each can reach into the mortal world; their stake (if any) in the metaplot.

---

## 9. The Draconic

**Dragons are real — and rare, ancient, and terrible** (Far / mythic-tier powers, seldom
seen and deeply feared). **Dragonborn** are a proud, uncommon people **bound to dragons by
old blood or origin**; they hail from **Kharak** but also hold **ancient city-states on
Acente** that long predate the human Empire (which is why settlers count them as "native").

**▸ To develop:** kinds of dragons and where they sleep; dragonborn culture and their
relationship to true dragons; whether kobolds share the draconic line.

## 10. History & the Elder Age

What is **commonly known**:
- Humans **abandoned the dying Frozen North** and crossed to **Acente**, where over
  generations they spread and forged the **Empire**.
- The frontier is dotted with **ruins of an elder age** — older than the Empire, older than
  the settlers' memory. *What built them, and why they fell, is not commonly known.*

The deep truths — the death of the North, the origin of magic, what sleeps in the ruins,
and what the Empire is *truly* after — are **not yet known** and live in `dm-secrets/`
(`hidden-lore.md`, `empire-metaplot.md`), to be earned through play.

**▸ To develop:** a rough timeline/ages; the elder age's identity; dates and the calendar
(see `world/calendar.md`).

## 11. Technology & Society

- **Late-medieval** technology overall.
- **Gunpowder exists but is very rare** and takes **odd, regional forms** — there are **no
  standardized firearms**. (Mechanics: `rules/skills.md` › *Powder-arms*.)
- **Coin & trade:** Empire-minted coin and settler scrip circulate; tribes often prefer
  barter. (See `inventory/currency-and-assets.md`.)

**▸ To develop:** notable regional powder-forms; trade goods and routes; daily life on the
frontier.

## 12. Languages

- **Common** is the trade tongue of the frontier. Each people keeps its own language(s).

**▸ To develop:** language families and which peoples share them; written scripts; which
