# Character Creation — Run This First

> **DM:** If `character/character-sheet.md` is still a blank template, run this
> walkthrough with the player **before any story begins.** Go one step at a time,
> conversationally — ask, react, suggest, and only move on when the player is happy.
> Reference `rules/attributes.md` and `rules/skills.md` as you go. When finished, write
> the result into `character/character-sheet.md` and begin Session 1.

The player is a **newcomer to the frontier**, arriving as the campaign opens. They should
end creation **competent but unproven** — clearly capable, with a couple of real
strengths, but no legend yet. There is room to grow for years.

Walk the player through these steps.

---

### Step 1 — Concept & Background
Ask the open questions, in your own words:
- Who are they? A name, and a one-line sense of the person.
- **Where do they come from, and why have they come to the frontier?** (Fleeing
  something? Seeking fortune, land, knowledge, a person? Sent? Exiled?)
- What did they *do* before — trade, soldier, scholar, smuggler, noble's runaway?
- What do they look like, and how do they carry themselves?

Let this conversation suggest their attributes and skills rather than forcing numbers first.

---

### Step 2 — Core Attributes
Explain the scale briefly (10 = average; they'll cap around 14–16 as a newcomer).

**Method:** Each of the six core attributes — **STR, DEX, CON, INT, CHA, WIL** — starts
at **8**. The player has **a pool of 30 points** to add, **+1 per point**, with **no
single attribute above 16 at creation.**

- This yields someone with a couple of standout 14–16 strengths and the rest solidly
  human — *gifted in their niche, unproven overall.*
- Encourage at least one deliberate weakness (leaving something at 8–9) for character.
- Then set **Luck** somewhere in **3–6** based on their story (a charmed life vs. a
  hard-luck past). Default 4.

Read the relevant band descriptions back to them so the numbers feel concrete.

---

### Step 3 — Skills
Using the list in `rules/skills.md`, assign starting proficiencies:
- **1 Journeyman** skill — their defining competence, the thing they're genuinely good at.
- **3 Apprentice** skills.
- **5 Novice** skills.
- Everything else is **Untrained**.

Make sure the picks fit the background from Step 1. A frontier scout looks different from
a runaway scribe. **No starting magic** (it's discovered in play) — but note if their
story leaves a door open to it.

---

### Step 4 — Languages, Tools & Gear
- **Languages:** Common (the trade tongue) plus **one** more; **+1 additional if INT ≥ 14.**
- **Tools:** **two** tool proficiencies fitting their trade.
- **Starting gear:** a sensible kit for who they are — clothing, a weapon or two they're
  trained with, travel basics, and one or two **personal items** that matter to them.
  Record in `inventory/items.md`.
- **Starting coin:** modest — roughly **15 gold crowns'** worth, adjusted by background
  (a destitute refugee less; a merchant's child more). Record in
  `inventory/currency-and-assets.md`. (Currency defined there.)

---

### Step 5 — Edges & Flaw
- **One or two Edges** — signature advantages: a knack ("never forgets a face"), an asset
  (a loyal horse, a contact in a port town), a bit of fortune, a piece of mysterious
  heritage. The DM may turn an Edge into a future hook.
- **One Flaw** — a real liability that will bite in play: a debt, an enemy, a vice, a
  fear, a secret, a wanted name. This makes failure and drama land.

---

### Step 6 — Ties to the World
- **One connection** already in the frontier — someone or somewhere they know, however
  thin (a cousin in a settlement, a name on a letter, a creditor). The DM seeds this as
  a starting thread.
- **One personal goal** — what would make this whole gamble worth it for them? (This is
  the player's *north star*; the metaplot is the world's.)

---

### Step 7 — Finalize
Write the complete sheet into `character/character-sheet.md` using its template, confirm
it with the player, then open **Session 1**: their arrival on the frontier. Make the
first scene small and grounded — a road, a gate, a dock, a wary stranger — and hand them
their first choice.
